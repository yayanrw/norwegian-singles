Which status are you?
Recreational
1–3 runs/week, mostly for fitness. No structured plan. FTHR is approximately 85% of max HR.
Trained
4–5 runs/week with a training plan, racing occasionally. FTHR is approximately 88% of max HR.
Elite
6+ runs/week, high volume, structured periodization. FTHR is approximately 91% of max HR.
A higher ratio means your threshold is a larger fraction of your max — less headroom above it. When in doubt, choose Trained.
How to perform the FTHR test
1
Choose your route. Flat track, flat road, or treadmill. Hills and traffic introduce pacing variability that skews the result.
2
Use an external chest strap (HRM). Wrist-based HR sensors are unreliable at high intensities. A chest strap gives significantly more accurate data for this test.
3
Warm up 10+ minutes — easy run + dynamic stretching or running drills. Rest 2–3 min before starting the test effort.
4
Run as hard as you can sustain for 20 minutes. Start conservatively for the first 5 min, then decide every 5 min if you can maintain or push slightly harder. Most people go too hard early and fade — a fading effort gives inaccurate data.
5
Record the average HR for the full 20 minutes from your watch or HRM.
6
Enter your average HR in the stepper above. The app automatically computes your FTHR (avg × 0.95) and generates your zones.
Example: avg HR 185 bpm → app computes FTHR = 176 bpm.
No TT available? Use recent 10k race average HR as a rough proxy.
When to retest
SituationAction
Every 4–6 weeks during active training Retest — fitness improves, FTHR rises, zones shift upward
After a race with HR data race avg HR × 0.95 as a quick updated proxy
Returning from injury or a break Retest before resuming quality sessions — FTHR will have dropped
Heat, illness, or heavy fatigue Skip — HR is artificially elevated, results will be inaccurate
This aligns with the Norwegian Singles principle: race or 5k time trial every 4–8 weeks to update training zones.
